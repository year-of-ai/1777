# Gluck's Armide

**Category**: Arts & Culture  
**Key figures**: Christoph Willibald Gluck (composer), Philippe Quinault (librettist), Torquato Tasso (source author), Rosalie Levasseur (soprano, original Armide), Marie Antoinette (royal patron)

## Summary

Christoph Willibald Gluck's *Armide* premiered on September 23, 1777, at the Académie Royale de Musique in Paris. Gluck composed this opera—his fifth and final production for the Paris Opera—by setting Philippe Quinault's celebrated libretto virtually unchanged, a libretto that had originally been set to music by Jean-Baptiste Lully in 1686. Based on episodes from Torquato Tasso's *Gerusalemme Liberata* (*Jerusalem Delivered*, 1581), the work follows Armide, a Syrian sorceress, who attempts to destroy the Crusader knight Renaud through enchantment but instead falls catastrophically in love with him. Gluck's bold decision to restore and reinvigorate a canonical French libretto—rather than commissioning original dramatic text—challenged the conventions of the day and sparked one of the most celebrated cultural controversies of the eighteenth century.

Gluck himself considered *Armide* his favorite among all his compositions. The premiere starred Rosalie Levasseur (c. 1749–1826) in the title role, one of the foremost dramatic sopranos in France. Marie Antoinette, who had been Gluck's pupil in Vienna before her marriage to the Dauphin, attended the premiere and remained an ardent champion of the composer throughout the controversy that followed.

## The Opera: Plot and Structure

*Armide* is a *tragédie en musique* in five acts, following the structural conventions of French Baroque opera established by Lully. The action draws on Cantos XIV–XVI and XX of Tasso's epic poem:

- **Act I**: Armide, sorceress and niece of the king of Damascus, receives tribute for her victories over the Crusaders—except for the knight Renaud, who has escaped her every trap. She secretly desires and fears him.
- **Act II**: After a battle, Renaud lies sleeping on the banks of a river. Armide approaches with a dagger to kill him, but finds herself unable to strike. Overcome by love, she transports him by enchantment to her palace.
- **Acts III–IV**: Armide's enchanted gardens hold Renaud captive. Two of his comrades, Ubalde and the Danish Knight, break the spell using a magic shield that reflects Renaud's honor back to him.
- **Act V**: Renaud awakens, duty and glory assert themselves over love, and he departs with his companions. Armide, abandoned, destroys her enchanted palace and flees.

Gluck's setting preserves Quinault's poetry with minimal alterations, adding only an introductory scene and a ballet sequence. The opera's celebrated "monologue" in Act II—"Ah! si la liberté me doit être ravie"—in which Armide hesitates over the sleeping Renaud, was singled out by contemporaries as one of the most psychologically penetrating pieces of dramatic music yet composed.

## Gluck's Reform Operas

*Armide* belongs to the final phase of Gluck's systematic campaign to reform operatic practice. Beginning with *Orfeo ed Euridice* (Vienna, 1762) and *Alceste* (Vienna, 1767), Gluck had argued—most explicitly in the preface to the published score of *Alceste*—that music must serve poetry and drama rather than subordinate them to the display of singers' virtuosity. His principles: overtures should foreshadow the drama; arias should flow naturally from the action; da capo repetitions should be eliminated; recitative should be musically expressive rather than merely functional; and the chorus and dance should be integral to the drama.

His Paris operas before *Armide*—*Iphigénie en Aulide* (1774), *Orphée et Eurydice* (1774, a French reworking of *Orfeo*), and *Alceste* (1776, French revision)—had already tested these principles on Parisian audiences. *Armide* represented a different kind of challenge: rather than a new drama, it recycled a text already famous in the Lully setting, inviting direct comparison between the Baroque and "reformed" styles.

## The Gluckistes-Piccinnistes Controversy

The premiere of *Armide* inflamed a cultural debate that had been simmering since 1776, when Gluck's rival Niccolò Piccinni (1728–1800) was brought to Paris by opponents of Gluck's reform aesthetic. The resulting *querelle des Gluckistes et Piccinnistes* (c. 1776–1779) became one of the most intense aesthetic controversies in French cultural history:

- **Gluck's partisans** included most of the philosophes and progressive critics, arguing that music should be a vehicle for dramatic truth and emotional expression. The critic Jean-François Marmontel and the librettist Jean-François de La Harpe were prominent voices, and Marie Antoinette's visible patronage lent the cause aristocratic prestige.
- **Piccinni's defenders** favored the Italian lyrical tradition—melodic elegance, singer-centered performance, and entertainment over dramatic intensity. The abbé Arnaud and the journalist Ginguené advanced Piccinni's cause in rival pamphlets and periodical articles.
- The controversy spilled into the streets: Parisian salons split into rival factions, pamphlets multiplied, and newspapers published elaborate point-by-point rebuttals. One contemporary observer estimated that hundreds of documents were circulated in connection with the dispute during the three-year controversy.

Piccinni himself reportedly admired Gluck's genius and was embarrassed by the public contest waged in his name. When both composers were eventually commissioned to set the same libretto (*Iphigénie en Tauride*)—Gluck in 1779, Piccinni in 1781—Gluck's version was universally judged superior, effectively ending the controversy.

## Reception and Legacy

Despite the fierce intellectual debate surrounding it, *Armide* was not among Gluck's most commercially successful Paris premieres. Critical reception was divided: admirers praised the psychological depth of Act II's sleeping scene and Armide's final lament; detractors found the work dramatically static compared to the more action-driven *Iphigénie* operas.

The opera nonetheless remained continuously in the Académie Royale de Musique's repertoire until 1826—nearly fifty years—providing a living link between the French Baroque and the Romantic era. The critic and composer Hector Berlioz, writing in the 1830s–1850s, gave the work some of his warmest praise, calling Gluck's setting a masterpiece of dramatic truth and ranking the Act II monologue among the supreme achievements of operatic writing. Berlioz's advocacy helped cement *Armide*'s canonical status in the nineteenth century.

The opera was revived in Paris at the Opéra-Comique in 1905 (with a celebrated production involving Désirée Artôt de Padilla) and has been staged periodically since, with notable productions at the Opéra de Paris in the late twentieth century.
