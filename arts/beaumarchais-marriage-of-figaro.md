# The Marriage of Figaro (Composition in 1777)

**Category**: Arts & Culture  
**Key figures**: Pierre-Augustin Caron de Beaumarchais, King Louis XVI, Marie Antoinette, Mozart, Lorenzo da Ponte

## Summary

Pierre-Augustin Caron de Beaumarchais wrote *La Folle Journée, ou Le Mariage de Figaro* (The Marriage of Figaro) as a sequel to *The Barber of Seville* during 1777–1778, while simultaneously managing covert arms shipments to the American Revolutionary forces through his *Roderigue Hortalez et Compagnie* company. King Louis XVI personally banned the play after a private court reading in 1777–1778, objecting to its direct attack on aristocratic privilege and royal authority. The play's famous Act V monologue, in which Figaro denounces the nobility, crystallized Enlightenment critique of the ancien régime. The work remained suppressed until 1784, when it premiered on April 27 at the Comédie-Française to unprecedented acclaim, becoming the highest-grossing theatrical production of the 18th century.

## Beaumarchais in 1777: Dual Roles

The year 1777 found Beaumarchais operating simultaneously on two revolutionary fronts. As playwright, he was completing a five-act comedy begun in 1775 that subjected aristocratic privilege to sustained satirical attack. As covert arms dealer, he was directing the supply network he had established with French government backing in late 1775 — the fictitious trading house *Roderigue Hortalez et Compagnie* — through which he shipped approximately 25,000 muskets, 200 cannons, 200,000 pounds of gunpowder, and uniforms for 30,000 soldiers to the Continental Army by year-end 1777. These supplies reached American forces in time for the Battles of Saratoga (September–October 1777), the turning-point engagement that persuaded France to enter the war formally.

Beaumarchais drew personally and artistically on these experiences: the comedy's central conceit — a clever servant outmaneuvering an aristocratic master — mirrored his own position as a watchmaker's son who had penetrated the French court and turned it against itself. His *Barber of Seville* had premiered February 23, 1775 at the Comédie-Française; *The Marriage of Figaro* extended the same characters into explicitly political territory, heightening the stakes at precisely the moment French society was most alert to questions of liberty and hierarchy.

## The Play's Structure and Censored Content

*The Marriage of Figaro* comprises five acts staged over a single tumultuous day at the Château d'Aguas-Frescas. Its primary targets are the *droit du seigneur* (the aristocratic claim to sexual access over servants' brides) and hereditary privilege. The five-act structure presents Count Almaviva's scheming as simultaneously comic and politically menacing, with the servants — Figaro and Suzanne — out-thinking their employer at every turn.

The censorship crisis centred on Act V, Scene 3, in which Figaro delivers a 600-word soliloquy attacking the social order directly:

> *"You took the trouble to be born — nothing more. Beyond that, you're a fairly ordinary man! Whereas I, lost in the crowd, have had to deploy more knowledge and calculation merely to survive than has been expended governing all of Spain for the past hundred years!"*

Louis XVI read the manuscript in 1777–1778 and declared that "the Bastille would have to be torn down" before such a play could be performed. He personally cancelled a performance scheduled for June 13, 1783 at the Menus-Plaisirs theatre after approximately 3,000 noblemen and court figures had already obtained tickets — an act of royal prerogative that paradoxically increased the play's notoriety. The manuscript circulated widely in private readings among the French aristocracy and intelligentsia during the suppression years 1778–1784, building a audience already intimately acquainted with every line.

## Suppression, Private Readings, and the 1784 Premiere

The six-year period of suppression was not passive silence. Beaumarchais organized private readings for influential audiences, deliberately cultivating demand. Marie Antoinette herself hosted readings at Versailles; the paradox of the queen sponsoring a play attacking aristocratic privilege escaped no observer. The play was submitted to royal censors repeatedly — Beaumarchais later claimed five separate censorship reviews, each resulting in demands for revision that he nominally accepted but effectively ignored.

When Louis XVI finally permitted the premiere on April 27, 1784, the event produced one of the most dramatic theatrical responses of the century. Carriages began queuing before the Comédie-Française at dawn; several people were reportedly injured in the crowd. The production ran 68 consecutive performances — the longest run and highest box office of any play in 18th-century France — and generated revenue that Beaumarchais donated to charity in an explicit public gesture of political disinterest. Contemporary accounts record that sections of the Act V soliloquy were applauded line by line.

## Revolutionary Reception and Legacy

The play's revolutionary impact was recognized immediately by those who lived through both the cultural premiere and the political revolution it anticipated. Georges Danton later characterized the play as having "killed off the nobility"; Napoleon Bonaparte, reviewing the decade in retrospect, called it "the Revolution already put into action." Radical journalists throughout the 1784–1789 period cited Figaro's monologue as a template for their own attacks on privilege.

Mozart and Lorenzo da Ponte adapted the libretto for their operatic version *Le nozze di Figaro*, which premiered in Vienna on May 1, 1786. Da Ponte and Mozart retained the political structure while softening some of Figaro's most inflammatory lines to satisfy Emperor Joseph II's censors — yet the opera became the vehicle through which the play's arguments reached German-speaking Europe and eventually the concert halls of the 19th century. The opera's four-act structure compressed Beaumarchais' five acts while preserving the core inversion: servants are wiser and more morally coherent than their masters.
