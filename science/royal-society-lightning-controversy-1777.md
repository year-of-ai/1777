# The Royal Society Lightning Conductor Controversy of 1777

**Event**: A major scientific and political dispute within the Royal Society over the design of lightning conductors, culminating in the resignation of the society's president

**Date**: 1777

**Key Figures**: Benjamin Franklin (inventor of pointed lightning rods, 1749), Benjamin Wilson (advocate for rounded conductors), Sir John Pringle (Royal Society president, who resigned over the controversy), King George III

## Summary

In 1777, the Royal Society became embroiled in a bitter dispute over whether lightning conductors should have pointed tips (as invented by Benjamin Franklin) or rounded tops (as championed by Benjamin Wilson, an accomplished painter and natural philosopher). The controversy erupted after a powder magazine in Purfleet, Essex, protected by Franklin-style pointed rods, suffered minor damage following a lightning strike. Wilson seized on this incident to argue that rounded conductors would be more effective and safer. The dispute rapidly transcended science, becoming a proxy for political allegiances during the American Revolutionary War — supporters of Franklin's pointed conductors were accused of favoring America, while proponents of Wilson's design were seen as loyal to the British crown. The controversy eventually forced Sir John Pringle, the Royal Society's president and a defender of Franklin's design, to resign, marking a remarkable moment when scientific disagreement became entwined with international politics and nationalist sentiment.

## The Scientific Background

Benjamin Franklin's pointed lightning rod, invented in 1749 and described in his landmark 1751 essay "Experiments and Observations on Electricity," had been widely adopted throughout Britain and Europe by the 1770s. Franklin's experiments and theory suggested that pointed conductors would discharge atmospheric electricity safely before lightning could strike, protecting buildings and infrastructure. The technology had proven effective enough that Britain's government, beginning in the 1770s, referred important scientific questions to the Royal Society's council for expert advice.

The Purfleet powder magazine itself had been protected by Franklin-designed rods after a Royal Society committee — which Franklin himself served on — recommended them in 1772. The committee had specifically chosen pointed conductors based on Franklin's theoretical framework that electrically sharp points create a "silent discharge" of atmospheric electricity, reducing the likelihood of a dangerous lightning strike.

However, Benjamin Wilson — a Fellow of the Royal Society, accomplished portrait painter (he is known to have painted David Garrick and other luminaries), and self-taught natural philosopher — harbored doubts about Franklin's theory. Wilson had published his own electrical experiments beginning in the 1750s and held a rival theory: pointed conductors did not quietly discharge electricity but rather attracted lightning strikes by concentrating the electrical field. He advocated instead for blunt or domed conductors that he believed would diffuse electrical charge more harmlessly across a broader surface.

## The Purfleet Powder Magazine Incident

On May 15, 1777, lightning struck the Purfleet powder magazine despite its Franklin-style pointed rods, damaging part of the structure. The strike was minor — no explosion occurred and casualties were avoided — but Wilson interpreted it as decisive evidence for his position: the Franklin rods had failed to prevent the strike. He presented this argument to the government and the Royal Society, calling for a formal review of lightning protection policy.

The Purfleet incident brought the lightning conductor debate to the attention of His Majesty's Government, which sought the Royal Society's expertise to settle the matter. The government's concern was practical and urgent: powder magazines represented critical military infrastructure, and a magazine explosion could be catastrophic. With Britain fighting a colonial war in America while managing a global empire, secure ammunition storage was a matter of national security.

## Wilson's Dramatic Pantheon Demonstration (1777)

In 1777, Benjamin Wilson constructed one of the most spectacular experimental apparatuses of the 18th century: a 47-meter-long model suspended by silk threads from the ceiling of London's Pantheon, a fashionable assembly hall on Oxford Street. The device — made of sheets of tin foil and pasteboard — could generate and direct electrical discharges to model the behavior of lightning striking a building. At one end stood a miniature replica of the Purfleet arsenal.

Wilson used this device to demonstrate before King George III and assembled observers that his blunt, rounded conductors protected the Purfleet model from simulated lightning strikes more effectively than Franklin's pointed rods. The demonstration was theatrical and designed to convince both the scientific community and the crown. The spectacle drew considerable attention — a scientific argument conducted at heroic scale in a fashionable entertainment venue underscored how deeply the controversy had moved beyond laboratory debate into public spectacle.

The demonstration was also politically calculated: by presenting directly to King George III rather than through the Royal Society's normal deliberative process, Wilson deliberately bypassed the scientific community's peer review in favor of royal authority. His willingness to appeal directly to the king underscored how high the political stakes had become.

## The Political Transformation

What began as a legitimate scientific disagreement rapidly became a matter of national politics and imperial allegiance. Opponents of Franklin's design began to associate support for pointed conductors with sympathy toward America and the American Revolution. In the charged atmosphere of 1777 — when the Continental Army had defeated Burgoyne at Saratoga (October 17) and France was preparing to enter the war — support for any idea associated with Benjamin Franklin became politically suspect in British circles.

Franklin, then serving as American envoy to France and the chief architect of the [Franco-American alliance](../economics/franco-american-diplomacy-1777.md), was simultaneously one of Britain's most celebrated scientists and its most dangerous diplomatic opponent. His lightning rod design now carried the weight of that dual identity: to endorse "Franklin's points" was, in the eyes of some Britons, to endorse the man who was engineering Britain's military defeat.

A satirical epigram of the period captured the mood:

> *While you, great George, for knowledge hunt,*
> *And sharp conductors change for blunt,*
> *The nation's out of joint;*
> *Franklin a wiser course pursues,*
> *And all your thunder useless views,*
> *By keeping to the point.*

The epigram, attributed to various authors including the scientist and poet David Rittenhouse, mocked George III for letting political vanity override scientific reasoning — and gained wide circulation on both sides of the Atlantic.

Conversely, those who championed Wilson's rounded conductors positioned themselves as loyal subjects defending the British crown. The scientific question of whether points or domes were more effective became secondary to questions of patriotism and political allegiance.

## The Resignation of Sir John Pringle

Sir John Pringle (1707–1782), the president of the Royal Society since 1772 and a respected natural philosopher and physician who had served as physician-general to the British Army, sided with Franklin's pointed conductor design on scientific grounds. A friend and correspondent of Franklin, Pringle had supported the 1772 Purfleet committee's recommendation of pointed rods and maintained that position despite the Purfleet strike.

As the controversy intensified and opposition to anything "American" or associated with Franklin grew more virulent, Pringle came under attack. In conversations at Court, King George III reportedly suggested that Pringle, as president of the Royal Society, should use his authority to endorse blunt conductors. Pringle famously replied: "Sire, I cannot reverse the laws of nature." Unable to reconcile his scientific convictions with royal pressure and the accusations against his loyalty, Pringle resigned as president of the Royal Society in November 1778. He was succeeded by Joseph Banks, who would lead the Society for over four decades.

Pringle's resignation marked a dramatic moment when scientific principle — the freedom to evaluate evidence and theory on merit — collided with wartime patriotism and political pressure. It represents one of the earliest documented cases of a scientist choosing professional integrity over political accommodation at significant personal cost.

## Aftermath and Scientific Resolution

Following Pringle's resignation, the British government ordered the installation of blunt conductors at Purfleet and other powder magazines. Wilson's position temporarily prevailed in official British policy — a triumph of political pressure over scientific evidence.

The controversy faded after the Revolutionary War ended in 1783. Scientific consensus gradually returned to Franklin's theory as experimental evidence accumulated. Benjamin Franklin himself never directly engaged in the public controversy; from Paris, he reportedly remarked that the British government could choose blunt conductors if it wished, but that lightning made no distinction based on nationality.

Modern understanding has entirely vindicated Franklin's basic theory: pointed conductors provide superior protection by creating a low-impedance path to ground and concentrating the electric field to encourage leader attachment. The 1777 episode is now cited by historians and philosophers of science as a case study in the sociology of scientific knowledge — how political context can override empirical evidence in the short term while the scientific consensus ultimately prevails.

## Significance

The 1777 lightning conductor controversy at the Royal Society illuminates several crucial aspects of the American Revolutionary War and late-18th-century science:

**The Politicization of Science**: The episode reveals how the American Revolution permeated every aspect of British intellectual and political life, even reaching into specialized scientific disputes. Scientific merit alone could not determine the outcome of the lightning conductor debate; political allegiance had become decisive.

**Franklin's Global Influence**: Despite his absence from Britain (he was in France negotiating the French alliance), Benjamin Franklin's scientific reputation was potent enough to inflame British opinion and divide the Royal Society. His authority as a natural philosopher made him a symbolic figure in the broader conflict — a man whose ideas were fought over even in their purely technical applications.

**The Limits of Scientific Authority**: The Royal Society, founded on the principle of evaluating nature through experiment and reason, found itself unable to resolve the lightning conductor question on scientific grounds alone. The Society's credibility as an impartial arbiter of science suffered when its president was forced to resign for his scientific conclusions.

**Royal Interference in Science**: The episode prefigured later debates about the relationship between state authority and scientific independence. King George III's reported pressure on Pringle became a cautionary tale cited in arguments for scientific autonomy from political influence.

**Long-term Resolution**: The controversy was eventually settled after the war ended. Modern understanding has vindicated Franklin's basic theory: pointed conductors do provide superior protection by offering a low-resistance path to ground. However, the 1777 episode demonstrated that during wartime, scientific truth is not always victorious over political passion — at least not immediately.
