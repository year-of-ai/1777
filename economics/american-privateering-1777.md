# American Privateering During the Revolutionary War

**Category**: Economics & Trade
**Key figures**: Continental Congress (commissioning authority), Benjamin Franklin (diplomat leveraging privateers against Britain), Silas Deane (Congressional envoy coordinating European operations), Lambert Wickes and Gustavus Conyngham (most celebrated privateer captains)

## Summary

American privateering became a primary economic weapon of the Revolutionary War, operating at peak intensity through 1777. The Continental Congress and individual states commissioned privately-owned vessels through "Letters of Marque and Reprisal," granting them legal authorization to seize enemy shipping and distribute the captured prizes among crew and investors. These commissions—which distinguished lawful privateering from common piracy—created a powerful incentive structure: unlike Continental Navy sailors, who surrendered prizes to the government at fixed rates, privateers kept all captured goods and vessels.

By 1777, when General George Washington commanded approximately 11,000 Continental troops, roughly the same number of men served simultaneously as privateers on the Atlantic, in the Caribbean, and even in British home waters near the English Channel and the Irish Sea. Over the course of the war the Continental Congress issued roughly 1,700 Letters of Marque and individual states added hundreds more; together with these commissions, more than 2,000 vessels operated as privateers, ranging from small 8-ton sloops to substantial 600-ton, 26-gun ships. The most common vessels were nimble two-masted schooners and brigantines valued for speed over firepower.

## Scale of Operations in 1777

The economic toll on Britain escalated sharply in the first months of 1777. By February, American privateers had captured approximately 250 British West Indian merchant ships; the losses triggered the collapse of four major West India merchant companies in London. British maritime insurance rates, which had been 2–3% in peacetime, climbed to 25–30% for trans-Atlantic voyages to North American waters by mid-1777, effectively pricing many merchants out of that trade.

Over the entire war, American privateers captured an estimated 2,300 British vessels—more than ten times the roughly 200 prizes taken by the official Continental Navy. Contemporary estimates put the cumulative value of captured British ships and cargoes at approximately $18 million, a sum that far exceeded the cost of outfitting and crewing the privateer fleet.

Two captains became especially prominent in 1777. Lambert Wickes, commanding the 18-gun *Reprisal*, conducted an audacious raid through the Irish Sea in early 1777—the first such American naval operation in British home waters—capturing at least 18 prizes before diplomatic pressure from Britain forced France to expel him from Nantes. Gustavus Conyngham, operating from Dunkirk under a Continental commission, took over 60 prizes in British waters in 1777 alone, earning the nickname "the Dunkirk Pirate" in the British press.

## The Pirate Act and Britain's Legal Response

Parliament responded with the Pirate Act of 1777 (also called the Treason Act), passed in March of that year. The legislation stripped captured American privateers of prisoner-of-war protections, allowing them to be held indefinitely without trial and subjected to the same penalties as common pirates—including potential execution. The act was both a legal escalation and a measure of British desperation: conventional prisoner exchanges under the laws of war were being exploited to return experienced privateer crews to sea rapidly.

The Pirate Act, however, generated significant anti-war backlash within Britain itself. London merchants, already suffering from rising insurance rates and lost cargoes, resented a Parliament that seemed to be intensifying the conflict rather than resolving it. Opposition politicians, including Edmund Burke and Charles James Fox, cited the privateering crisis as evidence that the war was unwinnable at acceptable economic cost.

## Diplomatic Consequences

Benjamin Franklin, then serving as American minister to France, systematically exploited the privateer crisis to advance diplomatic objectives. Operating from Paris, he issued Continental commissions to European-based captains and encouraged French merchants to invest in privateer ventures, blurring the legal distinction between French neutrality and active assistance to America. Every privateer capture in British waters created a diplomatic incident that London had to manage simultaneously with conducting the war itself.

The privateering campaign was a direct factor in accelerating French entry into the conflict. French ministers had long sought a pretext to aid America without triggering an immediate British declaration of war; the demonstrated vulnerability of British commerce—graphically illustrated by raids in home waters—helped convince them that Britain was overextended and that the strategic moment to intervene had arrived. The Franco-American Treaty of Alliance signed in February 1778 followed directly from the diplomatic ground Franklin cultivated in part through the privateering threat.

## Significance

Privateering was the young republic's most effective instrument of economic warfare. Lacking a navy able to challenge the Royal Navy in fleet actions, the United States turned private capital and seafaring expertise into a decentralized maritime force that struck Britain where it was most exposed—its merchant trade. The campaign raised insurance costs, disrupted West Indian commerce, tied up British warships in convoy escort, and inflamed domestic British opposition to the war, all at little public expense. Its strategic legacy was twofold: it sustained the American war economy through prize revenue, and it helped create the conditions for the French alliance that ultimately decided the conflict. The practice also exposed an unresolved tension in the laws of war that the Treason (Pirate) Act of 1777 laid bare—the contested boundary between lawful belligerent commissioning and piracy.
