# Battle of Bennington

**Category**: Military Events  
**Key figures**: Brigadier General John Stark (New Hampshire militia), Colonel Seth Warner (Green Mountain Boys / Continental Army), Lieutenant Colonel Friedrich Baum (Brunswick), Lieutenant Colonel Heinrich von Breymann (Brunswick), Lieutenant General John Burgoyne (British northern army)

## Summary

The Battle of Bennington was fought on August 16, 1777, near Walloomsac, New York — approximately ten miles from the town of Bennington in the disputed New Hampshire Grants (present-day Vermont), whose supply depot gave the engagement its name. It pitted a force of New England and Vermont militia under Brigadier General John Stark against a detachment of German troops, Loyalists, Canadians, and Native American auxiliaries sent by British Lieutenant General John Burgoyne under Lieutenant Colonel Friedrich Baum. The result was a decisive American victory that stripped Burgoyne's advancing army of nearly a thousand men and much of the mobility it needed to reach Albany.

Burgoyne, pushing south from Canada along the Hudson corridor with an overextended supply line, dispatched Baum with roughly 700 men — including dismounted Brunswick dragoons of Prince Ludwig's regiment, British marksmen, Loyalists, Canadians, and about 100 Native American allies — to seize horses, draft animals, cattle, and provisions reported to be gathering at the Continental depot at Bennington. Stark, commissioned by New Hampshire and operating independently of the Continental command structure, had raised roughly 1,500 militia within days and marched to intercept the expedition. Learning of the militia's strength, Baum halted and entrenched on high ground above the Walloomsac River to await reinforcements.

On the afternoon of August 16, after morning rain delayed the assault, Stark enveloped Baum's fortified position from multiple directions, reportedly exhorting his men with words later remembered as "There are the Redcoats, and they are ours, or this night Molly Stark sleeps a widow." The German redoubt was overrun, and Baum was mortally wounded. As Stark's militia dispersed to plunder the captured camp, a German relief column of roughly 550–650 men under Lieutenant Colonel Heinrich von Breymann arrived and nearly reversed the outcome; the timely appearance of Colonel Seth Warner's Continental regiment and Green Mountain Boys allowed the Americans to rally and drive Breymann's force from the field at nightfall.

American losses were light — approximately 30 killed and 40 wounded. German, British, and Loyalist casualties were catastrophic: roughly 200 killed and some 700 captured, a loss of nearly the entire two-part detachment.

## Prelude

By August 1777 Burgoyne's campaign to sever New England from the other colonies had captured Fort Ticonderoga but stalled in the wilderness south of Lake Champlain, where felled trees, destroyed bridges, and long overland hauls from Canada left the army short of horses, wagons, and food. Intelligence — much of it exaggerated — indicated that the rebels had accumulated horses, cattle, and stores at Bennington guarded only by militia. Baum's expedition was therefore conceived as a foraging raid to remount the dragoons and provision the army rather than as a set-piece battle.

The American response was shaped by an unusual command arrangement. John Stark, a veteran of Bunker Hill and Trenton, had resigned from the Continental Army after being passed over for promotion, and accepted a New Hampshire militia commission only on the condition that he answer to the state rather than to Continental generals. This independence let him mass militia quickly and choose to strike Baum's isolated column.

## The Battle

Baum, recognizing that he faced far more than a militia screen, entrenched on a hill on the west bank of the Walloomsac and sent word to Burgoyne for reinforcements. Stark's plan exploited the dispersed and nationally mixed nature of Baum's position: detachments worked around both flanks and to the rear while a frontal force fixed the defenders. Many of the encircling militiamen were mistaken by the Loyalist and Native pickets for friendly countrymen until they opened fire at close range.

The coordinated assault collapsed the German redoubt in roughly two hours of close fighting; Baum, attempting a saber-led breakout, was mortally wounded and his dragoons overwhelmed. The battle's second phase came when Breymann's relief column blundered into the disordered American ranks. Warner's fresh Continentals stiffened the line, and the Americans pressed Breymann — himself wounded — into a retreat that abandoned his artillery and much of his command.

## Aftermath

The two-stage engagement cost Burgoyne close to 1,000 irreplaceable soldiers and much of his best light infantry and German cavalry, none of whom could be readily replaced so far from Canada. The defeat shattered the confidence of his Native American and Canadian auxiliaries, many of whom abandoned the expedition, depriving Burgoyne of his scouts and screen at the moment he most needed them. The forage he had gambled on securing never materialized.

Emboldened by the victory, New England militia turned out in growing numbers to reinforce the gathering Continental army under Horatio Gates on the Hudson. Within a month the two armies met at the [Battles of Saratoga](battles-of-saratoga.md), where Burgoyne — weakened, unsupplied, and unsupported — was defeated and ultimately surrendered his entire army on October 17, 1777.

## Significance

Bennington is widely regarded as one of the decisive turning points of the Saratoga campaign and, by extension, of the Revolutionary War. Militarily, it removed roughly a sixth of Burgoyne's fighting strength before the campaign's climactic battles and denied him the transport and provisions on which his advance depended. Politically and psychologically, it demonstrated that hastily raised militia, well led and locally motivated, could destroy a professional European detachment in the field — a lesson that swelled the ranks converging on Saratoga. The battle also cemented the reputation of John Stark, later promoted to brigadier general in the Continental Army, and reinforced the fighting identity of the New Hampshire Grants that would become Vermont. Its outcome fed directly into the American victory at Saratoga that convinced France to enter the war as an open ally in 1778. The date of the battle, August 16, is still observed as Bennington Battle Day, a legal holiday in Vermont.
