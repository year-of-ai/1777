# Battle of Germantown

**Category**: Military Events  
**Key figures**: General George Washington (American Commander-in-Chief), General Sir William Howe (British Commander-in-Chief), Major General Nathanael Greene (American), Major General John Sullivan (American), Brigadier General Anthony Wayne (American), Major General Adam Stephen (American), Brigadier General John Armstrong (Pennsylvania militia), Lieutenant Colonel Thomas Musgrave (British, 40th Regiment of Foot), Major General Charles Cornwallis (British)

## Summary

The Battle of Germantown, fought on October 4, 1777, was a major engagement of the Philadelphia campaign in which General George Washington attempted to surprise and destroy the main British encampment at Germantown, then a village roughly five miles northwest of Philadelphia (today part of the city). Coming just over three weeks after his defeat at [Brandywine](battle-of-brandywine.md) and barely a week after the British [occupation of Philadelphia](british-occupation-philadelphia.md), the attack reflected Washington's determination to seize the initiative. Howe had divided his force, detaching troops to garrison Philadelphia and to reduce the American forts on the Delaware River, leaving roughly 9,000 men encamped at Germantown against which Washington marched approximately 11,000 Continentals and militia.

Washington devised an ambitious plan in which four columns, marching by separate roads through the night, would converge on the British camp at dawn and envelop it. The two main Continental columns — Sullivan and Wayne advancing down the Germantown Road in the center, and Greene and Stephen approaching the British right by the Limekiln Road — were flanked by militia under John Armstrong on the far right and William Smallwood on the far left. The plan required precise timing across some sixteen miles of converging night marches.

The assault opened successfully: Sullivan's column drove in the British pickets and pushed the forward light infantry back through the fog that had settled over the field. The advance then unraveled. A detachment of about 120 men of the British 40th Regiment of Foot under Lieutenant Colonel Thomas Musgrave barricaded themselves in Cliveden, the stone mansion of Pennsylvania jurist Benjamin Chew, and held out against repeated American assaults and ineffective light artillery fire. The decision to reduce the house rather than bypass it cost time, casualties, and momentum. Heavy fog combined with gun smoke reduced visibility to a few yards; Greene's wing arrived late by its longer route, and in the confusion the divisions of Wayne and Stephen mistook one another for the enemy and exchanged fire, throwing both into a panicked withdrawal. With ammunition running low and cohesion lost, Washington ordered a retreat as Cornwallis brought reinforcements up from Philadelphia.

American losses were approximately 152 killed, 521 wounded, and some 400 captured; British and Hessian losses were roughly 71 killed, 448 wounded, and 14 missing. Among the dead were the American Brigadier General Francis Nash and the British Brigadier General James Agnew. The British held the field, and the Continental Army withdrew, ultimately entering winter quarters at [Valley Forge](valley-forge.md) on December 19, 1777.

## Order of Battle

**American forces (General George Washington):** approximately 11,000 total (about 8,000 Continentals and 3,000 militia), advancing in four converging columns
- Center: Major General John Sullivan and Brigadier General Anthony Wayne, with Washington — the main assault down the Germantown Road
- Left wing: Major General Nathanael Greene and Major General Adam Stephen — the larger column, approaching the British right via the Limekiln Road
- Far right (flank): Brigadier General John Armstrong, Pennsylvania militia, advancing by the Ridge Road against the British left
- Far left (flank): Brigadier General William Smallwood and Colonel David Forman, Maryland and New Jersey militia, directed against the British rear

**British and Hessian forces (General Sir William Howe):** approximately 9,000 encamped at Germantown
- Forward light infantry and the 40th Regiment of Foot screening the Germantown Road, with Lieutenant Colonel Thomas Musgrave's detachment holding Cliveden
- Main body in camp, reinforced during the battle by troops brought up from Philadelphia under Major General Charles Cornwallis

## The Cliveden Stand

The defense of Cliveden — the Chew House — became the battle's defining episode. As Sullivan's column pushed forward, Musgrave withdrew about 120 men of the 40th Regiment into the thick-walled mansion and barred the doors and windows. Washington's officers debated whether to mask the position and continue the advance or to reduce it; on the counsel of artillery chief Henry Knox, who held that no fortified post should be left in the army's rear, Washington committed troops and light field guns to the assault. The cannon could not breach the masonry, and successive infantry charges were repulsed with heavy loss. The fighting around the house consumed time and ammunition, and its noise — echoing behind the leading American units — added to the confusion that the fog was already sowing among the converging columns.

## Aftermath

In the aftermath, Washington moved the army first to Pennypacker's Mills and then through a series of encampments before settling at [Valley Forge](valley-forge.md) for the winter. Major General Adam Stephen, whose division had become disordered and had fired on Wayne's men, was court-martialed for misconduct — including a charge of intoxication during the action — and dismissed from the army; his troops were placed under the Marquis de Lafayette. Howe's army retained possession of Germantown and Philadelphia but remained dependent on opening the Delaware River, where the American forts at Mud Island (Fort Mifflin) and Red Bank (Fort Mercer) held out until November, prolonging the British supply crisis that Howe's capture of the capital had been meant to relieve.

## Significance

Although Germantown was a clear tactical defeat, contemporaries and historians have emphasized its moral and strategic effect. That Washington could mount a large, coordinated offensive against the regular British army only weeks after losing his capital signaled that the Continental Army remained a credible fighting force. European observers took note: coming so close to the news of the American victory at the [Battles of Saratoga](battles-of-saratoga.md) (October 17, 1777), Germantown reinforced the impression at the French court that the Americans would be persistent and capable allies — an assessment that fed the diplomatic momentum culminating in the [Franco-American alliance](../economics/franco-american-diplomacy-1777.md) of February 1778.

The battle also exposed the limits of the Continental Army at this stage of the war. Washington's four-column plan demanded a precision of timing and coordination that the army's staff and communications could not yet reliably deliver, and the fog, friendly fire, and the costly diversion at Cliveden showed how quickly an ambitious scheme could disintegrate under battlefield friction. The experience contributed to the case for the systematic training the army would undergo at Valley Forge under Baron von Steuben the following winter.
