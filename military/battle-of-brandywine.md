# Battle of Brandywine

**Category**: Military Events  
**Key figures**: General William Howe (British Commander-in-Chief), General George Washington (American Commander-in-Chief), General Wilhelm von Knyphausen (Hessian), General Charles Cornwallis (British), Major General Nathanael Greene (American), Brigadier General Anthony Wayne (American), Marquis de Lafayette (French volunteer, American staff)

## Summary

The Battle of Brandywine, fought on September 11, 1777, near Chadds Ford, Pennsylvania, was the largest single-day engagement of the American Revolutionary War and resulted in a decisive British victory that opened the road to Philadelphia. British General William Howe commanded approximately 18,000 troops against Washington's force of roughly 11,000 Continentals and militia. The action began at around 5 a.m. with Hessian General Wilhelm von Knyphausen pinning the American center at Chadds Ford with 6,800 men, while Howe led the main column of approximately 8,200 men on a 17-mile flanking march around the American right via Trimble's Ford and Jefferies Ford — a crossing Washington's scouts had reported unguarded.

Washington received multiple warnings of the flanking march during the morning, but contradictory intelligence from local guides and delays in confirming the threat caused him to hesitate until early afternoon. By approximately 4 p.m., Cornwallis's column emerged on high ground at Osborne's Hill and drove down into the American right and rear, held by divisions under Generals Sullivan and Stirling. The American right crumbled under the assault, and Washington committed his reserve — Nathanael Greene's division — to form a rearguard covering the retreat through the town of Dilworth. Greene's men, marching four miles in forty-five minutes, held the British long enough for the army to escape under darkness. Lafayette, serving as a volunteer aide-de-camp, was wounded in the left leg at Dilworth while helping rally fleeing troops.

American casualties numbered approximately 1,300 killed, wounded, or captured; British and Hessian losses totaled around 587. The Continental Army retreated northeast toward Chester, Pennsylvania, then toward Philadelphia, allowing Howe's forces to enter the colonial capital on September 26, 1777.

## Order of Battle

**British and Hessian forces (General William Howe):** approximately 18,000 total
- Left column (feint): General Wilhelm von Knyphausen — approximately 6,800 troops, attacked across Chadds Ford
- Right column (main assault): General Charles Cornwallis with Howe — approximately 8,200 elite troops, executed the flanking march via Jefferies Ford

**American forces (General George Washington):** approximately 11,000 total
- Center: General Anthony Wayne, holding Chadds Ford against Knyphausen's demonstration
- Right wing: Generals Sullivan, Stirling, and Stephen — positioned on the Birmingham Meeting House heights
- Reserve: General Nathanael Greene's division — committed as rearguard at Dilworth

## Tactical Analysis

Howe deliberately replicated his successful double-envelopment from the Battle of Long Island (August 27, 1776), where he had similarly sent the main body around the American left while a smaller force fixed the defenders in place. Washington's failure to adequately patrol and picket Jefferies Ford — approximately 8 miles above his right flank — left the army vulnerable to a flanking march that was anticipated but not adequately guarded.

The battle demonstrated both the strengths and weaknesses of the Continental Army at this stage of the war. American infantry fought with courage at the Birmingham Meeting House, holding their ground for approximately ninety minutes against superior British forces before the line broke. Greene's division's rapid march and disciplined rearguard action — praised by Washington as the action that saved the army — showed the growing professionalism of the Continental forces. Baron de Kalb, the German officer serving with the American army, later noted that Greene's march covered terrain that British observers described as physically impossible in the time allotted.

Brandywine also marked a formative experience for the nineteen-year-old Marquis de Lafayette, wounded in his first engagement. Washington wrote to the Continental Congress: "The Marquis de la Fayette was wounded in the leg, and General Woodford in the hand. Notwithstanding the misfortune of the day, I am happy to find the troops in good spirits; and I hope another time we shall compensate for the losses now sustained."

## Aftermath

The defeat immediately threatened Philadelphia, the seat of the Continental Congress and the most important city in America. The Congress evacuated Philadelphia on September 18, 1777, moving to Lancaster and then York, Pennsylvania. Howe occupied Philadelphia on September 26 — see [British Occupation of Philadelphia](british-occupation-philadelphia.md).

Washington counterattacked at the Battle of Germantown on October 4, 1777, demonstrating that the army had recovered its offensive spirit, though the assault — complex and ambitious — was ultimately repulsed. The army then entered winter quarters at [Valley Forge](valley-forge.md) on December 19, 1777. Brandywine's preservation of the Continental Army as a coherent force proved strategically critical: had Howe destroyed Washington's army rather than merely defeating it, the war's outcome might have differed fundamentally. Instead, the war continued until the concurrent [Battles of Saratoga](battles-of-saratoga.md) (October 17, 1777) brought France formally into the conflict, shifting the strategic balance against Britain.

## Significance

Brandywine is significant on three levels. Militarily, it was the largest single-day engagement of the Revolutionary War in terms of troops engaged and exposed critical deficiencies in American intelligence and flank security. Politically, it forced the evacuation of the Continental Congress and the fall of the colonial capital — a major symbolic and practical blow to the American cause. Strategically, however, Washington's successful extraction of the bulk of his army preserved the war's continuation. Historian David Hackett Fischer has observed that Washington's ability to withdraw under pressure, maintaining unit cohesion, was a defining strategic skill that ultimately proved more valuable than battlefield victory. Lafayette's wounding and subsequent bond with Washington, forged at Brandywine, shaped the French-American alliance and the eventual campaign that culminated at Yorktown in 1781.
