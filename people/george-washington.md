# General George Washington

**Category**: Key Figures
**Key figures**: George Washington; Marquis de Lafayette; Baron von Steuben; General Horatio Gates; General William Howe; Thomas Conway

## Summary

George Washington served as Commander-in-Chief of the Continental Army throughout 1777, a year of profound trials and ultimate transformation. The year opened with strategic promise following the victories at Trenton (December 26, 1776) and Princeton (January 3, 1777), but quickly entered a cascade of defeats and deprivations. Washington lost the critical [Battle of Brandywine](../military/battle-of-brandywine.md) on September 11, 1777, to British General William Howe, suffering approximately 1,300 casualties — killed, wounded, and captured — against British losses of roughly 587. The defeat opened the road to Philadelphia. Five days later, a British night attack dispersed part of his army at the Battle of the Clouds (September 16). Unable to mount a successful defense of the revolutionary capital, Washington was forced to watch British forces under General Charles Cornwallis occupy Philadelphia on September 26, 1777.

Washington's attempted counterattack at the [Battle of Germantown](../military/battle-of-germantown.md) on October 4, 1777, briefly succeeded — multiple American columns pierced British lines — but a combination of morning fog, a misfired signal, and American units firing on each other turned near-victory into an expensive retreat. Washington lost approximately 1,100 more men at Germantown.

On December 19, 1777, Washington led an army of approximately 11,000 men into winter encampment at Valley Forge, Pennsylvania, 18 miles northwest of Philadelphia. By the time the army broke camp in June 1778, between 2,000 and 3,000 soldiers — estimates vary — had died, primarily from typhus, typhoid fever, dysentery, and pneumonia rather than from cold. The crisis was compounded by a broken supply system: on December 23, 1777, Washington wrote to Congress that he had 2,898 men "unfit for duty because they are barefoot and otherwise naked."

Yet within this crucible, Washington's leadership proved transformative. He cultivated alliances with European volunteers like the [Marquis de Lafayette](marquis-de-lafayette.md) and Baron von Steuben, reorganized the Continental Army's structure, and successfully navigated a political challenge from officers and congressmen who questioned his command.

## The Philadelphia Campaign: Brandywine to Germantown

Washington's 1777 operational plan was to defend the middle colonies — above all Philadelphia — while Burgoyne's army descended from Canada to divide New England from the southern states. Howe's decision to advance by sea through the Chesapeake rather than overland through New Jersey forced Washington into an unfavorable defensive position at Brandywine Creek. The September 11 battle saw Howe feint at Chadd's Ford while sending a column of 8,000 men on a wide flanking march to strike Washington's unguarded right flank. Washington received reports of the flanking column but acted on them too late. Despite the defeat, his army remained intact and cohesive — a testament to the discipline he had built over two years of command.

The [British occupation of Philadelphia](../military/british-occupation-philadelphia.md) on September 26 forced the Continental Congress to flee first to Lancaster and then to York, Pennsylvania. The loss of the revolutionary capital was a severe propaganda blow, though Philadelphia's strategic value was limited: Howe's army was now committed to garrisoning a city rather than destroying Washington's field army.

Washington's Germantown attack on October 4 demonstrated his aggressive instincts. Despite its failure, the battle convinced French observers — including the Chevalier de Chastellux reporting to Versailles — that the Continental Army retained offensive capability. This assessment reinforced Benjamin Franklin's lobbying in Paris and contributed to the diplomatic momentum that the Saratoga news would soon accelerate.

## Valley Forge: Crisis and Transformation

Washington's decision to winter at Valley Forge was deliberate: the position covered the approaches to Congress's new seat at York while remaining close enough to Philadelphia to monitor British movements. The site, however, offered little natural shelter. Soldiers constructed approximately 1,000 log huts — each 14 feet by 16 feet, housing 12 men — according to specifications Washington issued by general order. Washington himself occupied a stone farmhouse (the Isaac Potts house) but pledged to remain in the field as long as his troops suffered in huts.

The army's near-collapse during the winter of 1777–1778 was not primarily caused by British attack but by a dysfunctional Continental supply system. Congress lacked the authority to compel state contributions; commissary officers lacked funds; roads were impassable in mid-winter. On December 22–23, Washington issued his famous protest to Congress detailing the army's condition. The letters galvanized a congressional response, and Nathanael Greene was appointed Quartermaster General in February 1778, beginning the logistical reform that restored the army.

Baron Friedrich Wilhelm August Heinrich Ferdinand von Steuben arrived at Valley Forge on February 23, 1778, and immediately began drilling companies of soldiers in European infantry tactics — close-order drill, bayonet use, and synchronized volley fire — that the Continental Army had lacked. His work, commissioned by Washington, transformed a militia-style force into a professional army by spring. Alongside Lafayette's divisional leadership and Greene's supply reforms, Washington presided over the most significant institutional transformation of the Continental Army.

## The Conway Cabal

In the autumn and winter of 1777–1778, Washington's command faced a political challenge that historians debate in scope and seriousness. A letter from General Thomas Conway to General Horatio Gates (the victor of Saratoga) disparaging Washington's abilities was leaked to Washington in November 1777. Washington confronted Conway directly and informed congressional allies. The episode revealed genuine congressional frustration with Washington's defensive campaign but also demonstrated his skill at political maneuvering: he handled the crisis with restraint, allowing his enemies' lack of a credible alternative to defuse the threat. Conway resigned in April 1778. The episode illuminated the civil-military tensions inherent in republican command and Washington's mastery of them.

## Significance

Washington's 1777 experience redefined his historical role from regional commander to indispensable leader of the American cause. His defeats at Brandywine and Germantown, though militarily costly, preserved his army's core strength and demonstrated resilience that prevented British conquest of the rebellion's heartland. More crucially, his steadfast command during Valley Forge's horrors — maintaining discipline, morale, and purpose despite starvation and disease — proved his mettle in the war's darkest hour.

His political survival during the Conway Cabal showed he could navigate the civil-military tensions that threatened to fracture the revolutionary government. Perhaps most importantly, Washington's 1777 struggles indirectly catalyzed French intervention: the news of [Burgoyne's surrender at Saratoga](../military/battles-of-saratoga.md) reached Paris in December and directly triggered the French alliance treaties of February 1778. [Benjamin Franklin's](benjamin-franklin.md) parallel diplomatic campaign in Paris depended on Washington keeping an army in the field. By creating the conditions — and bearing the defeats — that proved American arms could prevail, Washington's 1777 became the fulcrum upon which the entire war's outcome balanced.
