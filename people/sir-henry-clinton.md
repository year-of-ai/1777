# Sir Henry Clinton (1730–1795)

**Category**: Key Figures
**Key figures**: Henry Clinton, Sir William Howe, George Washington, Lord Germain, King George III, General John Burgoyne

## Summary

General Sir Henry Clinton (1730–1795) was a British army officer who served throughout the American Revolutionary War and was Commander-in-Chief of British forces in North America from 1778 to 1782. Having spent part of his childhood in the colony of New York, where his father was Royal Governor, he rose through the Seven Years' War and arrived in America in 1775 as one of three major generals sent to reinforce General Thomas Gage.

In 1777 Clinton was passed over for the northern command given to Burgoyne, was awarded the Order of the Bath as a consolation, and served as Howe's second-in-command in New York. He warned presciently of Burgoyne's vulnerability and launched a belated Hudson River offensive that arrived too late to prevent the Saratoga surrender. As Commander-in-Chief he captured Charleston in 1780 but feuded with Lord Cornwallis, whose autonomous operations led to the decisive defeat at Yorktown.

## Early Life and Military Formation

General Sir Henry Clinton, KB (16 April 1730 – 23 December 1795) was born in Newfoundland, where his father, Captain (later Admiral) George Clinton, was serving as Royal Governor. When Admiral Clinton was subsequently appointed Governor of New York Province (1743–1753), the young Henry Clinton spent formative years in the colonies he would later be assigned to subdue — an irony frequently noted by his biographers. He entered the British Army and served in the Seven Years' War, gaining significant experience as aide-de-camp to Prince Ferdinand of Brunswick during the German campaigns, where he was present at the Battle of Minden on August 1, 1759 — the same engagement in which [Lord George Germain](lord-george-germain.md) earned his lasting disgrace for the failure of the British cavalry to charge. Clinton's own conduct at Minden was unimpeachable; the contrast between his behavior and Germain's at that battle would acquire an additional dimension of irony when Germain became Clinton's nominal superior during the American war.

Clinton was promoted to Major General in May 1772 and elected Member of Parliament for Boroughbridge the same year. He was a studious, intellectually engaged officer who kept extensive private papers, recording his observations, grievances, and strategic assessments throughout his career — documents now held at the William L. Clements Library at the University of Michigan and constituting one of the richest primary sources for the British perspective on the Revolutionary War.

## Arrival in America and Early Campaigns

Clinton arrived in Boston on May 25, 1775, sent as one of three Major Generals (with John Burgoyne and William Howe) to reinforce General Thomas Gage. At the Battle of Bunker Hill on June 17, 1775, Clinton played an active role, commanding the final assault wave and personally rallying troops when the earlier attacks stalled. He recognized that the British had won a pyrrhic victory at devastating cost — approximately 1,054 British casualties against 450 American — and pressed, unsuccessfully, for an immediate aggressive exploitation of the weakened American position.

In 1776, Clinton commanded the failed British assault on Sullivan's Island, South Carolina, in June — a naval-land operation intended to establish a southern base that was repulsed by the defenders of Fort Sullivan (later Fort Moultrie) under Colonel William Moultrie. Despite this setback, Clinton participated in the successful New York campaign later in 1776, contributing strategic ideas for the envelopment of American forces at Long Island (August 27, 1776) and the subsequent capture of New York City.

## 1777: Passed Over, Knighted, and Sidelined

The assignment of the 1777 northern campaign to [General Burgoyne](general-john-burgoyne.md) rather than Clinton was a significant blow. Clinton had sought command of the descending expedition from Canada, viewing it as the war's decisive strategic operation. King George III's preference for Burgoyne — perhaps influenced by Burgoyne's superior social connections and his personal lobbying in London — left Clinton in a subordinate and frustrating position. He sought to resign his commission in protest.

The Crown's response was characteristic: rather than grant the resignation or offer the command, King George III offered Clinton the prestigious Order of the Bath to retain his services. Clinton was formally invested on April 11, 1777, and sailed for New York on April 29. The knighthood, which he deserved on military merit, was simultaneously a consolation prize substituted for the command he had merited and sought.

Upon returning to New York, Clinton served as Howe's second-in-command with approximately 7,000 troops — too few, Clinton repeatedly insisted, for any serious offensive action, while Howe prosecuted the Pennsylvania campaign with the main British force. Clinton's strategic analysis proved prescient. He recorded his alarm at Burgoyne's vulnerable position in his private papers, warning prophetically: "I fear it bears heavy on Burgoyne ... If this campaign does not finish the war, I prophesy that there is an end of British dominion in America."

## The October 1777 Hudson River Offensive

As Burgoyne's situation at Saratoga became desperate in October 1777, Clinton made a belated effort to relieve him. On October 3, 1777 — at virtually the same moment Burgoyne was preparing his second attack at Bemis Heights — Clinton received reinforcements from Britain (approximately 1,700 men) that brought his available striking force to a viable level. He launched a rapid assault up the Hudson River on October 6, 1777, capturing Forts Clinton and Montgomery in a swift coordinated attack, then proceeding northward. British forces burned Kingston, New York — the provisional American capital — on October 16, 1777, one day before Burgoyne surrendered.

The timing was fatal. Clinton's forces under General John Vaughan reached Esopus (Kingston) on October 15–16 and sent scouts northward as far as Livingston Manor — approximately 45 miles south of Saratoga — before receiving word that Burgoyne had already capitulated. The proximity of relief that arrived a day too late became one of the Revolutionary War's most discussed near-misses. Clinton, characteristically, recorded his conviction that a larger striking force and earlier orders from Germain's office could have made the difference. Germain had delayed sending the reinforcements that enabled Clinton's October offensive; had they arrived in August rather than early October, the strategic situation might have been altered.

## Commander-in-Chief, 1778–1782

Following General Howe's resignation, Clinton succeeded to the supreme command of British forces in North America on May 11, 1778. His tenure as Commander-in-Chief was immediately complicated by the French alliance that had been precipitated by Saratoga — the very disaster he had warned against. The entry of France into the war on February 6, 1778, dramatically altered British strategic priorities: London now required Clinton to defend the Caribbean islands, which were of greater economic value to Britain than the American continent, diverting troops and ships that might otherwise have been used to press the American campaign.

Clinton's first major action as Commander-in-Chief was the evacuation of Philadelphia — captured by Howe at such cost to Burgoyne's northern campaign — on June 18, 1778. The overland march across New Jersey led to the Battle of Monmouth on June 28, 1778, where Clinton's rearguard successfully repulsed an American attack, though the British army continued its march to New York. His southern strategy yielded genuine successes: the capture of Savannah, Georgia, on December 29, 1778, and — his greatest personal military achievement — the capture of Charleston, South Carolina, on May 12, 1780, when an entire American army under General Benjamin Lincoln surrendered, yielding more than 5,000 prisoners. This was the largest single American defeat of the entire war.

Despite these successes, Clinton's relationship with his subordinate commander in the south, Lord Cornwallis, deteriorated severely. Disputes over strategy, credit, and authority led Cornwallis to pursue increasingly autonomous operations that culminated in the Yorktown campaign — conducted without Clinton's full approval and ending in the surrender of October 19, 1781 that effectively ended the war. Clinton requested relief from command in 1781 and was replaced by Sir Guy Carleton in April 1782.

## Legacy and Historical Assessment

Clinton spent the years after the war defending his record in a sustained public dispute with Cornwallis, publishing *The American Rebellion* (edited from his narrative papers) to counter Cornwallis's published campaign accounts. He served briefly as Governor of Gibraltar (1794–1795) and died at Gibraltar on December 23, 1795, aged sixty-five.

Historians have increasingly rehabilitated Clinton's reputation. Modern assessments identify him as arguably the ablest British commander available in America — perceptive, capable, and clear-eyed about both the challenges of his strategic situation and the institutional dysfunction of the British command structure. His 1777 prescience about Burgoyne, his Hudson River initiative, and his southern successes all reflect genuine military talent. The structural problems he confronted — ambiguous orders from [Germain](lord-george-germain.md), inadequate resources relative to strategic commitments, and the destabilizing effect of the [Franco-American alliance](../economics/franco-american-diplomacy-1777.md) on British strategic priorities — are now understood as the fundamental determinants of British failure, rather than any deficiency of Clinton's generalship.

## Significance

Sir Henry Clinton's role in 1777 encapsulates the structural problems afflicting British command in America. He was arguably the ablest overall commander available to Britain, yet was deliberately sidelined from the principal strategic decision — the assignment of Burgoyne to the northern campaign. His prescient warning about Burgoyne's vulnerability, made before the campaign began, proved tragically accurate but went unheeded. Clinton's desperate October attempt to support Burgoyne, though militarily competent, lacked the coordination and resources necessary to alter the outcome, arriving too late to prevent the disaster.

Clinton's knighthood in 1777, while a mark of honor, was also a consolation prize — a substitute for the command he had sought and merited. His tenure as second-in-command under Howe exposed what contemporary observers and historians have identified as a fundamental weakness in the British command structure: the absence of a unified strategic vision, poor communication between commanders, and the tendency of London — especially Secretary of State Germain — to issue contradictory orders that left field commanders operating at cross-purposes. Clinton's extensive private papers, preserved at the Clements Library, provide invaluable first-hand perspectives on British high command during the Revolutionary War and constitute a primary source of lasting scholarly importance.
